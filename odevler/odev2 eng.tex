\begin{enumerate}



\item \textbf{(Expected Value)} Two dice are rolled in a game of chance. If at least one die shows a $4$ or the sum of the two dice is $7$, the player wins $5$~TL; otherwise, they lose $3$~TL.
\begin{enumerate}
\item What is the expected win/loss for a single game?
\item If we pay $30$~TL before playing the game $10$ times, what is the expected total net win?
\end{enumerate}

\vspace{0.5cm}

\item \textbf{(Discrete Random Variable)} The grade received in a geography assignment is a random variable $X$. The probabilities are as follows:
\[
P(A)=0.18,\quad 
P(B)=0.32,\quad 
P(C)=0.25,\quad 
P(D)=0.07,\quad 
P(E)=0.03,\quad 
P(F)=0.15
\]
\begin{enumerate}
\item Show the cumulative distribution function (CDF) of $X$ in a table.
\item Calculate the probability of getting a grade higher than B.
\item Calculate the probability of getting a grade lower than C.
\end{enumerate}

\vspace{0.5cm}

\item \textbf{(Law of Total Probability and Bayes' Theorem)} In an automobile factory, body welds are performed by two different robots:
\begin{itemize}
\item \textbf{Robot A} performs 60\% of the total production, and \textbf{Robot B} performs 40\%.
\item 5\% of the welds performed by Robot A are defective.
\item 10\% of the welds performed by Robot B are defective.
\end{itemize}
Accordingly;
\begin{enumerate}
\item What is the \textbf{probability of detecting a defective weld} in a randomly selected body at the end of the line?
\item \textbf{Given that a defective weld is detected} in a randomly selected body at the end of the line, what is the probability that this defective weld was performed by \textbf{Robot B}?
\end{enumerate}

\newpage

\item \textbf{(Continuous Random Variable)} The probability density function for the continuous random variable $X$ is given as follows:
\[
f(x)=
\begin{cases}
c(x+1), & 1<x<3 \\
0, & \text{otherwise}
\end{cases}
\]
\begin{enumerate}
\item Find the value of the constant $c$ for $f(x)$ to be a valid probability density function.
\item Find the corresponding cumulative distribution function $F(x)$.
\end{enumerate}

\vspace{0.5cm}

\item \textbf{(Conditional Probability and Bayes)} It is known from past experience that the probability of encountering an adult over the age of $40$ with cancer in a certain region of the country is $0.05$. If,
\[
P(\text{True positive}) = 0.78
\]
and
\[
P(\text{False positive}) = 0.06
\]
then, what is the \textbf{probability that an adult over $40$ diagnosed with cancer actually has cancer}?
\end{enumerate}

\section*{\textbf{\color{TitleColor}Just Think: Discrete and Countably Infinite Random Variable (Not Graded)}}

In a society, the childbearing behavior of every family is modeled as follows: In each birth, the probability of the child being a boy or a girl is equal, and
\[
P(\text{Boy}) = P(\text{Girl}) = \frac{1}{2}
\]
It is also assumed that births are independent of each other. Each family continues to have children until the first boy is born; once the first boy is born, they stop having children.

Based on this, answer the following questions:

\begin{enumerate}
    \item[\textbf{a)}] Define the total number of children a family has over the sample space and explain why the sample space of this experiment is discrete and countably infinite.
    \item[\textbf{b)}] Let the random variable \(X\) represent the number of girls a family has. Find the probability mass function of \(X\).
    \item[\textbf{c)}] Calculate the expected value of the random variable \(X\).
    \item[\textbf{d)}] Using the fact that every family ends up with exactly one boy, compare the expected number of girls and the expected number of boys in the society in the long run.
    \item[\textbf{\color{ChoiceColor}e)}] \textcolor{ChoiceColor}{\textbf{Based on your results, comment on how the girl/boy ratio will form in this society in the long run.}}
\end{enumerate}
